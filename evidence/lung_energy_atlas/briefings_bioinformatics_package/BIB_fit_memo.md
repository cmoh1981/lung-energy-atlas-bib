# BIB Scope Constraint

Briefings in Bioinformatics is a review-oriented bioinformatics journal that prioritizes practitioner-facing synthesis, analytic frameworks, resource mapping, and worked methodological lessons over a standard original-research IMRAD submission built around one dataset's primary discovery claim. In the current frozen science state, LEMPA cannot be positioned as a pure translational validation paper because V1 is an honest negative baseline, V2 is `METHOD-FRAGILE NEGATIVE`, H4b is interpretable only with major caveats, and there is no external replication. The manuscript therefore fits BIB only if the submission is reconstructed as a generalizable bioinformatics resource about atlas-guided radiogenomics validation failure modes, with LEMPA serving as the worked case rather than the sole basis of novelty.

# Article-Type Strategy

Primary form: **Review with worked case study**.

This is the strongest BIB-aligned option because it allows the paper to open with a field-level problem that matters to practitioners, namely how to move from atlas-derived cellular states to patient-level radiogenomic validation without overclaiming, and then use LEMPA as the concrete case that demonstrates where such pipelines fail under realistic public-data conditions. A review-with-case-study structure also gives enough editorial room to foreground a validation-gate framework, failure-mode taxonomy, reporting checklist, and reproducibility map, which are the parts most likely to justify BIB interest despite the negative empirical outcome.

# What Must Change from JCR5 Draft

The JCR5 draft still reads as a high-discipline original research manuscript with an atlas layer, a validation layer, and a negative conclusion. For BIB, that center of gravity must change. The paper should stop reading like an original NSCLC study that happened to fail and instead read like a practitioner resource on atlas-guided radiogenomics validation design. That means moving away from a standard IMRAD argument, reducing emphasis on study-specific performance as the paper's main drama, and restructuring around why atlas-to-imaging translation is difficult, which validation gates are non-negotiable, how target rarity and deconvolution disagreement distort downstream interpretation, and what the LEMPA case teaches future investigators.

The introduction should no longer promise a translational bridge and then report its failure. It should instead state upfront that the manuscript addresses a recurrent bioinformatics problem and that LEMPA provides a transparent example of a plausible but method-fragile negative outcome. The results should be recast as a case-study section embedded inside a broader framework paper. The discussion should shift from defending the value of a negative study to extracting portable guidance for analysts, reviewers, and editors. H4b, if retained, must appear late and only as a caveated remainder inside a failed broader validation line.

# What Original Results Can Do

The original results remain useful, but only in a bounded role. They can anchor the worked example that demonstrates how an atlas-derived hypothesis can survive biological plausibility yet fail at the patient-linked validation stage because public paired cohorts, deconvolution stability, and target construction are inadequate. The atlas layer can still function as the motivating resource contribution, while the V1 and V2 outcomes can serve as empirical evidence for the validation-gate framework and failure-mode taxonomy.

What the original results cannot do is carry the manuscript as a conventional novelty claim that BIB should publish on the strength of the LEMPA analysis alone. They do not support a successful radiogenomic bridge, they do not support externalizable prediction, and they do not support rhetorical rescue through H4b. Their value is as a transparent cautionary benchmark showing what responsible closure looks like when a bioinformatics translation pipeline remains analyzable but fails robustness criteria.

# Desk-Rejection Risks

The highest desk-rejection risk is that the submission still looks like pure original research submitted to the wrong journal. If the title, abstract, section structure, or figure logic make editors think this is mainly an NSCLC atlas-plus-radiogenomics application paper, BIB is likely to reject before review because the frozen evidence state does not offer a strong enough original-study payoff.

The next risk is overclinical framing. If the paper sounds like biomarker development, translational imaging validation, or clinical readiness without external replication, the manuscript will appear mismatched both to BIB's scope and to its own evidence. A third risk is H4b overclaiming. Because H4b is the only positive-looking remainder, any visual or rhetorical inflation of that result will make the manuscript look like it is trying to rescue a negative line through a fallback-dependent association. A fourth risk is insufficient generalizable bioinformatics framework. If the paper does not deliver a clear validation architecture, failure-mode map, and reporting discipline that other groups can reuse, editors may conclude that the manuscript is simply a well-documented failed application rather than an indispensable practitioner resource.

# Final Recommendation

BIB remains appropriate only after a full reframing toward a review with worked case study. In that form, the manuscript can plausibly fit the journal because the durable contribution is no longer a claimed radiogenomic success but a practical bioinformatics resource on how atlas-guided radiogenomics should be validated, where it commonly fails, and how such failures should be reported without distortion. Without that reframing, BIB is not appropriate. With that reframing, it is still a credible target, but only if the manuscript's primary value is made clearly generalizable beyond the LEMPA case and the frozen negative evidence state remains visible from the first page onward.
