# From Cell Atlases to Radiogenomics: A Lung Energy Metabolism Case Study in Bioinformatics Validation Failure Modes

## Abstract

Cell atlases are increasingly used to define biologically plausible targets for imaging-linked molecular inference, but plausibility at atlas resolution does not guarantee patient-level validation. For practitioners, the main lesson is not that atlas-guided radiogenomics is impossible, but that it should be treated as a staged bioinformatics validation problem with explicit gates for target definition, deconvolution agreement, abundance stability, cohort linkage, and robustness surfaces. This review with worked case study uses the Lung Energy Metabolism Pathway Atlas (LEMPA) to show how a strong normal-lung atlas contribution can coexist with a negative translational validation line. LEMPA maps pathway-level energy metabolism across 584,944 lung cells spanning 50 annotated cell types and remains useful as a normal-lung reference resource. Its paired NSCLC radiogenomic branch, however, closed negatively under frozen public-data conditions. In V1, only 3/50 cell types reached the prespecified cross-validated threshold of r >= 0.30. In V2, the redesign remained analyzable but failed the intended agreement and predictive gates: the top-10 median agreement between deconvolution methods was -0.087, only 1/10 top-abundance cell types reached r >= 0.4, and only 1/4 collapsed modules passed the pooled cross-validation threshold. H4a and H4c were null, while H4b survived only as a narrow association-level remainder tied to a fallback-dependent target definition. The practical contribution of this manuscript is therefore a validation discipline for atlas-guided radiogenomics, together with a failure-mode map showing why negative benchmark outcomes are informative when they are reported transparently rather than rhetorically rescued.

## Key points

Cell atlas translation requires validation gates rather than bridge rhetoric. Deconvolution agreement is a prerequisite, not an optional sensitivity analysis. Rare or diluted cellular targets are weak imaging endpoints even when they are biologically compelling. Public paired cohorts are often sufficient for execution but insufficient for robust external validation. Negative benchmark outcomes remain useful when they define what cannot yet be claimed.

## 1. Introduction: why atlas-guided radiogenomics needs validation discipline

The central bioinformatics lesson of atlas-guided radiogenomics is that biological resolution and predictive resolution are different achievements. A single-cell atlas can identify compelling cell states, pathway structure, and organ-specific heterogeneity, but the moment those atlas-derived states are projected onto patient-linked imaging data, the problem changes. The analyst is no longer evaluating cell biology alone. The analyst is evaluating whether multiple data layers, each with their own uncertainty structure, remain coherent enough to support a falsifiable patient-level claim. This is why atlas-guided radiogenomics should be treated as a validation discipline rather than as a straightforward translation of molecular insight into imaging correlates.

That distinction matters because the field is especially vulnerable to optimistic bridge narratives. Atlas-derived targets can appear biologically persuasive, radiomics can appear richly quantitative, and public paired datasets can appear to provide a ready validation surface. In practice, however, the decisive failure points often sit upstream of any final predictive model. Target rarity can dilute recoverable signal. Deconvolution methods can disagree on the very quantities that imaging is supposed to predict. Cohort linkage can reduce nominal sample size to a much smaller analyzable set. Technical covariates and source heterogeneity can be too limited to support meaningful robustness checks. A manuscript that ignores these gates risks converting a plausible biological question into an overstated translational claim.

This article is written as a practitioner resource on those failure modes, using the Lung Energy Metabolism Pathway Atlas (LEMPA) as a worked example. The goal is not to present a successful lung cancer radiogenomic validation story. The goal is to show how a biologically informative atlas project encountered negative and method-fragile outcomes once its targets were tested against frozen public paired NSCLC data, and how those outcomes can be converted into reusable validation guidance. LEMPA is therefore most useful here as a case study in how a transparent negative benchmark can discipline future atlas-guided radiogenomic design.

## 2. Lung energy metabolism as a motivating case

Lung energy metabolism is a good motivating substrate for this discussion because it is distributed across structurally and functionally distinct cellular compartments rather than concentrated in one dominant lineage. Airway epithelial maintenance, immune surveillance, endothelial homeostasis, stromal support, and alveolar function all impose different bioenergetic demands. A normal-lung atlas should therefore reveal organized pathway heterogeneity rather than a single canonical metabolic profile. That expectation makes the atlas layer scientifically valuable on its own, independent of whether a patient-level imaging bridge can later be validated.

LEMPA was built around this atlas question. Using the integrated Human Cell Atlas lung resource, the project quantified glycolysis, pentose phosphate pathway activity, tricarboxylic acid cycle behavior, oxidative phosphorylation, and lactate-related metabolism across 50 annotated lung cell types. The resulting atlas showed pervasive pathway heterogeneity across the normal lung, with glycolytic enrichment concentrated in specialized airway epithelial and immune populations rather than collapsing onto one universal lung metabolic state. As a normal-lung resource, this is already a meaningful outcome because it provides a cell-type-resolved reference against which disease-linked metabolic claims can be interpreted.

The same biological richness, however, makes radiogenomic translation harder rather than easier. Imaging features operate at patient and lesion scale, whereas atlas-defined targets can be rare, composition-sensitive, and method-dependent. A target that is biologically coherent in a single-cell framework may still be poorly suited as an imaging endpoint if it is weakly abundant, unstable across deconvolution methods, or only indirectly represented in a mixed tumor sample. LEMPA therefore provides an instructive worked example precisely because the motivating biology was strong while the paired validation line remained fragile.

## 3. Data layers required for atlas-guided radiogenomics

Atlas-guided radiogenomics should be thought of as a multi-layer join problem before it is thought of as a modeling problem. At minimum, the analyst needs a well-characterized atlas resource, a strategy for deriving target signatures or fractions, bulk transcriptomic data that can be aligned to those targets, imaging features and masks derived under known quality procedures, patient linkage that preserves one-to-one correspondence, and enough metadata to evaluate whether robustness can be tested across meaningful technical strata. If any one of those layers is weak, the downstream interpretation narrows sharply.

The LEMPA case makes this point concrete. The atlas itself was large and biologically informative. The paired validation branch, however, did not operate on the nominal imaging cohort size alone. After quality control and inner joining radiomics with molecular and deconvolution artifacts, the executed paired analysis surface was 130 patients. That count was sufficient for analyzability, but it was not equivalent to a replication-ready cohort, and it did not guarantee a usable held-out robustness surface for site, batch, or histology testing. The practical lesson is that source cohort size, processed imaging size, paired size, and final analyzable size should be treated as separate quantities throughout design and reporting.

This is also where manuscript framing matters. A practitioner-focused paper should not present data integration as a hidden methods step that merely precedes the real science. In atlas-guided radiogenomics, integration quality is part of the scientific result because it defines the actual inferential surface. If the joint dataset is thin, technically imbalanced, or missing key metadata, then downstream claims about validation should be correspondingly modest, even when the modeling code runs cleanly.

## 4. Deconvolution as the first failure point

Deconvolution agreement is not optional in atlas-guided radiogenomics because it determines whether the imaging target remains stable enough to interpret. If two independent deconvolution strategies do not agree on the cell-type or module-level quantities that imaging is supposed to recover, any subsequent predictive performance estimate inherits an unresolved target-definition problem. In practical terms, this means that agreement should be treated as an early validation gate, not as a supplementary sensitivity analysis added after the main narrative has already formed.

LEMPA illustrates this point through the V2 redesign. V2 was not a failed run in the ordinary technical sense. The second deconvolution branch executed, produced non-degenerate outputs, and preserved an analyzable workflow. The failure emerged when agreement was measured. At the intended cell-type surface, the top-10 median agreement between the two methods was -0.087, and only 1/10 top-abundance cell types reached r >= 0.4. Those numbers do not describe mild method noise around a stable target. They describe a target definition that failed to hold together across methods strongly enough to support confident downstream interpretation.

The important consequence is conceptual rather than merely numeric. Once agreement fails at the cell-type surface, downstream modeling cannot be presented as if it were still testing the same target that motivated the study. The analyst may still proceed to a fallback representation for diagnostic or exploratory purposes, but the line has already changed status. In LEMPA, that change triggered collapse from higher-resolution cell-type targets to four broad modules. That fallback preserved analyzability, but it also marked a loss of target fidelity that had to remain visible in every later interpretation.

## 5. Target rarity and abundance dilution

Rare or weakly represented targets are intrinsically difficult imaging endpoints. Even when they are biologically interesting, they may contribute too little stable signal to be recovered from mixed tissue expression and lesion-scale radiomics. This is one of the most important general lessons for practitioners because the temptation to select the most biologically specific atlas target is often strongest precisely when that target is least likely to remain recoverable in paired patient data.

The V1 LEMPA result is an honest example of this difficulty. The preregistered success criterion required at least 10 cell types to reach cross-validated Pearson r >= 0.30. Only 3/50 cell types reached that threshold. This is not best read as near success or partial validation. It is best read as evidence that most intended cell-type endpoints were not recoverable under the available paired-data conditions. Whether the main problem was rarity, abundance dilution, target instability, or some combination of all three, the practical conclusion is the same: a biologically plausible target can still be a poor imaging endpoint.

This is also why target collapse must be interpreted carefully. Collapsing from many cell types to broad modules can improve analyzability because larger compartments are often more abundant and more stable across methods. But collapse also changes the scientific question. A module-level signal is not a rescued cell-type-level signal. It is a different target with different biological meaning. In the LEMPA line, that distinction is essential because the fallback structure improved executability without converting the study into a successful validation story.

## 6. Imaging feature reproducibility and cohort attrition

Even when target definition is biologically grounded, the imaging side of atlas-guided radiogenomics can narrow interpretation through ordinary but consequential forms of attrition. Radiomic feature extraction depends on segmentation quality, feature completeness, and patient linkage. Robustness claims additionally depend on whether the cohort contains enough technical heterogeneity to support meaningful stratified tests. A pipeline can therefore be analyzable at face value while still lacking the structure needed for a strong validation claim.

In LEMPA, the paired public NSCLC branch reached an executed analysis set of 130 patients, with the H4b analysis subset at 123 after routine missing-feature filtering. The relevant point is not that this count was unusably small. It is that the frozen local artifacts did not support a credible held-out robustness surface for the kinds of cross-source checks that would be needed to offset deconvolution fragility. Site information was effectively non-variable, and vendor information was limited. As a result, the project could not use the existing public line to demonstrate that any retained imaging association generalized across independent technical strata.

For practitioners, this means that imaging quality control and cohort structure should be reported as part of the validation logic, not as backend housekeeping. A study may succeed in generating radiomics, deconvolution outputs, and paired tables while still remaining too constrained for strong predictive language. The correct response is not to reinterpret the available association more optimistically. The correct response is to mark the robustness surface as absent and narrow the claim accordingly.

## 7. Validation gates: from plausible bridge to falsifiable benchmark

The most generalizable contribution of the LEMPA experience is a gate-based framework for atlas-guided radiogenomics. The first gate is executability: can the full paired pipeline run without degeneration? The second gate is agreement: do independent deconvolution strategies support a stable target surface? The third gate is target freeze: has the endpoint been fixed cleanly enough to prevent silent drift during evaluation? The fourth gate is predictive performance: does cross-validated association reach its prespecified threshold at the target surface that the study set out to test? The fifth gate is robustness: can the signal be challenged across meaningful technical or cohort splits? The sixth gate is residual interpretation: if a narrow association remains after broader failure, can it be described honestly without reversing the line-level outcome?

This framing is useful because it converts negative outcomes into structured information. A failed gate tells the field what was not achieved and where the translational bridge broke. That is more informative than a vague conclusion that the data were underpowered or that the biology was complex. It also disciplines future reopening criteria. A failed line should not be revived by optimism alone. It should be reopened only when a new paired cohort, restored method stack, or independent robustness surface directly addresses the gate that failed.

Under this framework, LEMPA functions as a falsifiable benchmark rather than a success narrative. V1 showed that the preregistered cell-type endpoint did not pass. V2 showed that a redesigned method stack remained analyzable but failed the intended agreement gate, triggered target collapse, and then failed the pooled module-level performance gate. The benchmark is useful precisely because these failures were archived transparently rather than blurred into exploratory flexibility.

## 8. LEMPA case study: V1 honest negative and V2 method-fragile negative

The worked example can therefore be summarized without ambiguity. V1 was an honest negative baseline. The prespecified B3 criterion required at least 10 cell types with cross-validated Pearson r >= 0.30, but only 3/50 cell types reached that mark. H4a and H4c did not produce supportive evidence in the frozen V1 outputs, and V1 H4b was not significant after false-discovery-rate correction. The correct interpretation is that the original patient-level validation line did not pass under its own preregistered criterion.

V2 was a redesign intended to test whether the V1 outcome reflected recoverable method constraints rather than an intrinsically weak translation surface. V2 remained technically analyzable, but the intended cell-type agreement path failed. The top-10 median agreement was -0.087, and only 1/10 top-abundance cell types reached r >= 0.4, forcing collapse to four broad modules. At that fallback surface, only 1/4 modules passed the pooled cross-validation threshold. These values matter because they show that the project did not move from V1 failure to V2 validation. It moved from an honest negative baseline to a method-fragile negative line whose target definition changed as agreement problems accumulated.

This is why the line-level classification must remain `METHOD-FRAGILE NEGATIVE`. The phrase captures two truths at once. First, the project was not invalidated by technical non-execution; the redesigned workflow ran and generated interpretable artifacts. Second, the resulting evidence did not support a method-robust positive claim. A practitioner paper should preserve both points because they distinguish a transparent negative benchmark from a mere failed analysis attempt.

## 9. Interpreting caveated residual associations such as H4b

Residual positive-looking associations are often the most rhetorically dangerous part of a negative line because they invite rescue narratives. LEMPA's H4b remainder is useful precisely because it shows how such a signal should and should not be handled. The result was reproducible within the frozen V2 tables, its sign pattern remained coherent after partial adjustment, and it did not appear to arise from duplicate joins or direct radiomic leakage. Those are real strengths. They justify reporting the association rather than discarding it.

At the same time, the H4b remainder cannot be allowed to reverse the broader conclusion. H4a was null, H4c was null, and the broad validation line remained negative at both the original and collapsed predictive surfaces. The retained H4b effects sit inside that failed line and depend on a changed target definition. In the frozen V2 audit, Benjamini-Hochberg FDR values were 3.93167135294686e-05 for entropy, 0.000192835821237218 for contrast, and 6.04179283005966e-05 for homogeneity. Those values can be reported, but they have to be interpreted as association-level evidence tied to variance across the four collapsed consensus modules rather than as confirmation of an unchanged cell-type heterogeneity endpoint.

This dependence is the critical caveat. The V1 H4b table used a higher-resolution weighted-capacity heterogeneity target, whereas V2 computed heterogeneity variance from the four collapsed consensus modules. The target redefinition materially strengthened the H4b associations. That means the most defensible statement is not that H4b validates atlas-guided radiogenomics in NSCLC. The most defensible statement is that a narrow texture-versus-heterogeneity association remained reproducible within a fallback-derived target inside an otherwise negative and method-fragile validation line.

## 10. Practical recommendations for practitioners

For analysts designing atlas-guided radiogenomic studies, the first recommendation is to freeze validation gates before interpretation becomes emotionally invested in the bridge. Agreement thresholds, abundance criteria, predictive cutoffs, and robustness expectations should be explicit before the paired analysis surface is explored. This reduces the risk that target collapse or fallback structures will later be narrated as ordinary refinement rather than evidence that the original target was unstable.

The second recommendation is to prefer targets that are abundant enough, stable enough, and biologically continuous enough across methods to remain interpretable at patient scale. Highly specific cell states may still be worth studying, but they should not automatically be treated as primary imaging endpoints. If a target is rare or method-sensitive, practitioners should consider whether a broader but more stable endpoint is scientifically preferable from the outset rather than after failure.

The third recommendation is to treat public paired cohorts as validation opportunities with known ceilings. Public resources are invaluable for execution, benchmarking, and transparent closure, but they often lack the sample structure or metadata needed for strong externalizable claims. Analysts should therefore plan for the possibility that a public-data line may end as an informative negative benchmark and define in advance what new data would justify reopening it.

The fourth recommendation is to report negative and method-fragile results as first-class outputs. The field benefits when a study states clearly that the atlas was useful, the paired workflow was analyzable, the deconvolution target failed agreement, and a residual association did not rescue the line. This kind of reporting is especially valuable in radiogenomics, where underreported fragility can otherwise create a distorted literature of apparent success.

## 11. Conclusions

LEMPA shows that cell atlases can produce biologically meaningful organ-specific resources while still failing to support a robust patient-level radiogenomic bridge under current public paired-data conditions. That is not a contradiction. It is a useful reminder that atlas quality and translational validation sit on different evidentiary tiers. The atlas layer remains informative as a normal-lung metabolic reference. The paired NSCLC branch remains valuable as a transparent negative benchmark that identifies where the bridge failed.

The durable contribution of the manuscript is therefore not a successful validation claim. It is a practical framework for how atlas-guided radiogenomics should be challenged before strong language is used: agreement must hold, target definitions must remain stable, predictive thresholds must be met on the intended endpoint, robustness surfaces must exist, and residual associations must stay subordinate when the broader line fails. By keeping LEMPA in view as a worked example, the paper turns one frozen negative case into generalizable guidance for future study design, interpretation, and reporting.
