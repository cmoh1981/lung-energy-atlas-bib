Dear Editor,

We submit for consideration our manuscript, "From Cell Atlases to Radiogenomics: A Lung Energy Metabolism Case Study in Bioinformatics Validation Failure Modes." This submission is designed for Briefings in Bioinformatics as a practitioner-facing bioinformatics resource: a validation framework and reporting guide for atlas-guided radiogenomics, anchored by the Lung Energy Metabolism Pathway Atlas (LEMPA) as a detailed worked case study.

The manuscript is not pitched as a lung cancer radiogenomic success story. Its main contribution is a generalizable framework for how investigators should test atlas-derived targets before making patient-level imaging claims. Using LEMPA, we show how biologically plausible atlas targets can fail at the validation stage when public paired cohorts are limited, deconvolution methods do not agree on the intended target surface, higher-resolution targets are rare or diluted, and robustness surfaces for external or cross-source confirmation are absent. We believe this problem is directly relevant to BIB readers because it is methodological, practical, and increasingly common as atlas resources are linked to imaging or other patient-scale data layers.

We also think the frozen negative evidence state strengthens, rather than weakens, the manuscript's value for practitioners. In the LEMPA case, V1 closed as an honest negative baseline, V2 remained analyzable but method-fragile, and the only retained association-level remainder, H4b, is explicitly subordinate and heavily caveated. Rather than trying to rescue these outcomes as translational success, the manuscript uses them to define failure modes that analysts, reviewers, and editors need to see reported clearly: deconvolution disagreement, target collapse, endpoint instability, cohort attrition, and lack of external validation surface. In that sense, the case study functions as a transparent cautionary benchmark and a reporting template for responsible closure.

For Briefings in Bioinformatics, the manuscript is framed not as pure original research but as a review with worked case study. It synthesizes the validation logic required for atlas-guided radiogenomics, maps the data layers and decision gates practitioners must document, and then uses the LEMPA NSCLC line to show what a negative but informative benchmark looks like in practice. The intended value is therefore broader than one disease application. We aim to provide a reusable validation architecture, a failure-mode taxonomy, and concrete guidance on how method-fragile negative outcomes should be communicated without overclaiming.

We have prepared the submission to keep its claims aligned to the frozen evidence. It does not claim successful radiogenomic validation, clinical readiness, or externalizable prediction. Instead, it argues that transparent negative benchmarks are necessary if atlas-guided radiogenomics is to mature as a credible bioinformatics discipline. We believe that combination of methodological synthesis, practitioner utility, and unusually explicit failure reporting makes the manuscript a strong conceptual fit for Briefings in Bioinformatics.
